% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
We document short-horizon cross-sectional return predictability in Vietnamese HOSE
equities. Training an XGBoost classifier on 35 technical and market-relative features
across 393 stocks with strict walk-forward validation (2015--2025), we find a mean
daily Spearman Information Coefficient (IC) of $+0.152$ and an annual net-of-cost
Sharpe ratio of $2.19$ for a concentrated top-5 square-weighted portfolio.
These performance figures should be interpreted cautiously given the portfolio's
concentration and the idealized execution assumptions described in
Section~\ref{sec:portfolio}.
The signal passes four pre-planned robustness tests: label permutation (IC collapses
to $\approx 0$), sector neutrality (IC drops only $8.8\%$ after within-sector
demeaning), mean-reversion decomposition (a simple contrarian rule accounts for
$33\%$ of model IC), and a one-day execution delay test (T+2 entry yields negative
Sharpe, confirming the edge is concentrated at the T+1 open).
We hypothesize that the T+1 open concentration reflects delayed cross-sectional
price adjustment driven by retail-dominated order flow and opening auction dynamics
on HOSE. A pre-committed 2026 out-of-sample test yields IC $= +0.134$, slightly
above the pre-committed range $[0.10, 0.13]$ and consistent with a weak-pass
interpretation: ranking skill remains intact, but realized net return remains
cost-sensitive in the partial-year holdout.
Data cutoff: 2026-03-18.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} cross-sectional return predictability, Vietnamese equities,
XGBoost, walk-forward validation, information coefficient, opening auction.

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

The Vietnamese Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE) is one of the fastest-growing
equity markets in Southeast Asia, yet it remains under-studied in academic finance.
With a market capitalization exceeding USD 200 billion and over 400 listed companies,
HOSE offers a rich laboratory for testing asset-pricing anomalies documented elsewhere.

Momentum --- the tendency for recent outperformers to continue outperforming ---
is one of the most robust anomalies in global equity markets
\citep{jegadeesh1993returns,rouwenhorst1998international,asness2014value}.
The classical momentum literature focuses on 6--12 month formation periods.
The present paper examines a shorter horizon: we study whether the \emph{relative
ranking} of stocks within a given week, as predicted by a machine-learning model,
carries predictive information for the following week's cross-sectional returns.
This is better characterised as short-horizon cross-sectional return predictability
than as classical momentum, and the mechanism we identify --- information
incorporation at the opening auction --- is distinct from the behavioural
under-reaction story underlying 6--12 month momentum.

This paper asks: does a machine-learning cross-sectional ranking model generate
stable out-of-sample alpha in Vietnamese equities over 2015--2026?
Our main contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item We demonstrate that an XGBoost classifier trained on 35 price-based features
        achieves mean IC $\approx +0.152$ across 11 out-of-sample test years,
        statistically significant ($p < 0.05$) in all 11 years.
  \item We subject the signal to four pre-planned robustness tests addressing data
        snooping, sector timing, mean-reversion overlap, and execution-timing
        sensitivity.
  \item We pre-commit to 2026 out-of-sample expectations before running the holdout,
        providing a prospective real-time test of signal stability.
  \item We provide a mechanistic hypothesis --- retail-dominated opening auction
        dynamics --- that connects the empirical pattern to known features of the
        HOSE microstructure.
\end{enumerate}

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews the literature.
Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the
methodology. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports main results.
Section~\ref{sec:robustness} covers robustness tests.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses practical implications and limitations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

% ── 2. Related Work ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\citet{jegadeesh1993returns} established the 12-1 month momentum anomaly in US
equities, documenting that past 6--12 month winners outperform losers by
$\approx 1\%$ per month. \citet{rouwenhorst1998international} extended this to 12
European markets. \citet{asness2014value} demonstrated value and momentum across
asset classes and geographies, including emerging markets.

In Asia, \citet{chui2010individualism} found momentum is weaker in East Asian markets
(China, South Korea, Japan) but present in Southeast Asia, attributing the difference
to cultural individualism proxies. Vietnam's market structure --- retail-dominated,
with a relatively immature institutional investor base --- may generate stronger
short-horizon predictability than the 6--12 month effects studied in that literature.

Machine learning for equity prediction has grown substantially.
\citet{lopez2018advances} provides a framework for backtesting ML strategies with
walk-forward cross-validation to prevent look-ahead bias, and introduces the
triple barrier labeling method used here.
\citet{chen2016xgboost} introduced XGBoost, which has become a standard baseline
for tabular financial data due to its robustness to overfitting.

Cross-sectional factor investing treats alpha generation as a ranking problem
\citep{asness2013devil}. We combine both perspectives: we train a binary classifier
but evaluate it through the ranking lens using Spearman IC, which is standard
practice in quantitative equity research \citep{lopez2018advances}.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{HOSE Stock Universe}

We collect daily OHLCV data for 393 HOSE-listed stocks from the VCI data source
via the vnstock Python library. The data spans 2012-01-01 to 2026-03-18.
Stocks with fewer than 300 trading days of history are excluded from any given
year's training pool, preventing thin-data stocks from distorting the signal.
Table~\ref{tab:universe} summarizes coverage by year.

\input{../output/tables/tab_universe}

\subsection{Benchmark Index}

The VN-Index (VNINDEX) serves as the market benchmark. All stock returns are
computed as excess returns relative to the VNINDEX, removing broad market beta
from both features and the target variable.

\subsection{VIX Filter}

We use the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX), sourced via yfinance, as a
portfolio-construction filter. Positions are only initiated on days where
$15 \leq \text{VIX} \leq 30$. Days with VIX below 15 are associated with
complacent trending regimes in which cross-sectional signal strength historically
degrades; days with VIX above 30 are associated with liquidity stress in which
execution assumptions become unreliable.

\subsection{Training / Test Split}

We use strict chronological walk-forward validation with no look-ahead:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Training:} all stock-days from years strictly prior to the test year
        (expanding window)
  \item \textbf{Validation:} last $12\%$ of training rows (chronological) for
        XGBoost early stopping only --- not used for model selection
  \item \textbf{Test years:} 2015--2025 (walk-forward), 2026 (pre-committed holdout)
\end{itemize}

No 2026 data is used in any training or hyperparameter selection step.

% ── 4. Methodology ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Feature Construction}
\label{sec:features}

We construct 35 features for each stock-day observation, computed entirely from the
stock's own OHLCV history and the VNINDEX close price. All features are stationary
(ratios and returns, not price levels). Table~\ref{tab:features} summarizes the
feature groups.

\input{../output/tables/tab_features}

\subsection{Triple Barrier Labeling}
\label{sec:labeling}

We follow the triple barrier method of \citet{lopez2018advances} adapted for
cross-sectional use. The target variable $y_{i,t}$ for stock $i$ on day $t$ is:

\[
y_{i,t} =
\begin{cases}
  1 & \text{if } \Delta_{i,t,j} \geq +1\% \text{ for some } j \leq 5 \\
  0 & \text{if } \Delta_{i,t,j} \leq -1\% \text{ for some } j \leq 5 \\
  \mathbf{1}[\Delta_{i,t,5} > 0] & \text{timeout (fixed 5-day direction)}
\end{cases}
\]

where $\Delta_{i,t,j} = r_{i,t+j} - r_{\text{VNI},t+j}$ is the stock's excess
return versus VNINDEX at day $t+j$. The $\pm 1\%$ barrier reduces label noise from
the drift component, producing sharper class separation relative to a simple
fixed-direction binary label \citep{lopez2018advances}.

\subsection{Cross-Sectional XGBoost Walk-Forward}
\label{sec:model}

For each test year $T \in \{2015, \ldots, 2025\}$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Pool all stock-days from years $< T$:
        $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} = \{(x_{i,t}, y_{i,t}) : \text{year}(t) < T\}$
  \item Split chronologically: last $12\%$ of $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ becomes
        validation data for early stopping
  \item Train XGBClassifier with early stopping on validation log-loss
  \item Score all stock-days in year $T$:
        $\hat{p}_{i,t} = P(y_{i,t}=1 \mid x_{i,t})$
  \item Compute daily Spearman IC:
        $\text{IC}_t = \text{Spearman}(\hat{p}_{\cdot,t},\, y_{\cdot,t})$
\end{enumerate}

XGBoost hyperparameters: $n_{\text{estimators}}=500$, $\text{max\_depth}=3$,
$\text{lr}=0.05$, $\text{subsample}=0.8$, $\text{colsample\_bytree}=0.8$,
early stopping rounds $= 30$.
These were selected based on exploratory analysis on pre-2015 data and then frozen
prior to the first test year; they were not revisited or adjusted at any subsequent
point. The depth-3 constraint in particular is important for preventing the model
from memorising within-year sector patterns that do not generalise across years
(see Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}).

The mean daily IC across $N_T$ trading days in year $T$ is reported as the primary
summary statistic. Statistical significance uses a one-sample $t$-test:
$t_T = \bar{\text{IC}}_T / (\hat{\sigma}_T / \sqrt{N_T})$.

\subsection{Portfolio Construction}
\label{sec:portfolio}

Each trading day $t$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Apply VIX filter: skip if $\text{VIX}_t \notin [15, 30]$
  \item Select top-$N=5$ stocks by predicted probability $\hat{p}_{i,t}$
  \item Require all $\hat{p}_{i,t} \geq 0.55$ (minimum confidence threshold)
  \item Compute square weights:
        $w_i = (\hat{p}_{i,t} - 0.5)^2 \,/\, \sum_j (\hat{p}_{j,t} - 0.5)^2$
  \item Execute at $T+1$ open (entry), exit at $T+2$ open
  \item Portfolio gross return:
        $R_t = \sum_i w_i (r_{i,t+1}^{\text{open}} - r_{\text{VNI},t+1}^{\text{open}})$
  \item Net return: $R_t^{\text{net}} = R_t - 0.30\%$ (round-trip cost)
\end{enumerate}

The $0.30\%$ round-trip cost ($0.15\%$ per side) reflects retail brokerage fees
on HOSE. We note two important caveats: (1) the portfolio is concentrated in
five positions, which inflates Sharpe relative to a diversified fund; (2) the
model assumes full fills at the opening price, which may not hold for illiquid
mid-cap names. The performance figures reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}
should be interpreted in light of these constraints.

\subsection{Label Permutation Test}
\label{sec:permutation}

We shuffle the actual labels independently of features and recompute IC.
The permuted IC collapses to $\approx 0$, consistent with the null hypothesis
of no predictive relationship. The original IC of $+0.1515$ is not an artefact
of the training procedure.

\subsection{Sector Neutrality}
\label{sec:sector}

We test whether the model primarily times sector rotations rather than picking
individual stocks within sectors. On each trading day, we demean the actual
outcome within each sector before recomputing Spearman IC.

We classify 167 of 393 tickers into 15 named sectors (Bank, Securities, Real
Estate, Steel, etc.); the remaining $57\%$ fall into ``Other''.
The sector-neutral IC drops only $8.8\%$ relative to the raw IC --- well below
the $15\%$ concern threshold. The model's predictive power is largely attributable
to within-sector stock selection rather than cross-sector timing.

\subsection{Mean-Reversion Decomposition}
\label{sec:mr}

A simple mean-reversion rule --- ranking stocks by the negative of their recent
5-day excess return --- generates an IC of $\approx 0.05$, accounting for
approximately $33\%$ of the model IC of $0.1515$. The model captures economically
distinct information beyond what a na\"ive contrarian rule achieves, though some
mean-reversion component is encoded in the short-horizon relative return features.

\subsection{Execution Timing Constraint}
\label{sec:execution}

We compare two execution schemes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $E=1$: signal at $T$, enter at $T+1$ open, exit at $T+2$ open (baseline)
  \item $E=2$: signal at $T$, enter at $T+2$ open, exit at $T+3$ open (one-day delay)
\end{itemize}

The $E=2$ net Sharpe is negative. The alpha is concentrated in the gap between
signal generation and the $T+1$ opening price. While this is disclosed as a risk
rather than a virtue, we note it is consistent with our mechanistic hypothesis
(Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}): if the signal reflects information that is slowly
incorporated into HOSE opening prices due to retail order flow dynamics, then
a one-day delay is sufficient to miss the window entirely.

\subsection{2026 Out-of-Sample Holdout}
\label{sec:holdout}

Before running the 2026 holdout, we pre-committed to the following expectations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item IC: expected $[0.10, 0.13]$; threshold for concern $< 0.05$
  \item Net/trade: expected $[+0.00\%, +0.25\%]$; threshold for concern $< -0.20\%$
\end{itemize}

Running the model on 2026 data (389 tickers, 43 trading days, through 2026-03-18),
we obtain IC $= +0.1343$, slightly above the top of the pre-committed range but
clearly consistent with the expected magnitude of the signal. The corresponding
portfolio result is a weak pass rather than a clean pass: the holdout portfolio
remains gross positive, but net/trade is slightly negative at $0.30\%$ RT cost
in this partial-year sample. We therefore interpret the 2026 evidence as:
the signal remains alive in ranking space, but economic realization remains
cost-sensitive and should not be overstated.

The 2026 IC point estimate falls modestly above the upper bound of the
pre-committed range ($0.1343 > 0.13$). Given the short partial-year window,
we attribute this to sampling variability rather than to a meaningful change in
the signal's underlying strength.

% ── 7. Discussion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Mechanistic Hypothesis}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The execution timing result (Section~\ref{sec:execution}) is the most
economically informative finding in this paper. The complete collapse of alpha
at $E=2$ implies that the predictable component of cross-sectional returns is
fully incorporated into prices within the first trading session following signal
generation. We hypothesize this reflects two features of HOSE microstructure.

First, HOSE has a concentrated retail investor base that responds to prior-day
price patterns with a lag: investors who observe a stock's closing rank at day $T$
and place opening orders at day $T+1$ are the marginal price-setters in the opening
auction. This creates a predictable, short-lived price impact that the model captures.

Second, the opening auction mechanism on HOSE aggregates overnight limit orders
before continuous trading begins. A cross-sectional signal generated from prior-day
close data is effectively predicting the \emph{demand imbalance} in this auction.
Once that imbalance clears at the $T+1$ open, no residual signal remains for $T+2$.

This hypothesis is consistent with the sector-neutrality result: retail order flow
responds to individual stock performance, not to sector-level news, which explains
why the model retains nearly all of its IC after sector demeaning.
It is also consistent with the mean-reversion component: retail-driven contrarian
buying of prior-week losers is a well-documented pattern in emerging markets.

\subsection{Transaction Cost and Execution Realism}
\label{sec:costs}

The $0.30\%$ RT assumption ($0.15\%$ per side) reflects retail brokerage fees.
Figure~\ref{fig:cost} shows the frozen paper pipeline breaks even at
$\approx 0.58\%$ RT,
providing headroom. However, the execution model has known limitations.

The model assumes full fills at the opening price for all five positions. In practice,
opening auction liquidity on HOSE varies considerably: large-cap names such as VCB or
VIC typically absorb orders of several hundred million VND without significant
slippage, while mid-cap names may not. We do not model queue priority, partial fills,
or market impact, and caution that realized net returns at meaningful AUM will be
lower than reported. An execution feasibility analysis incorporating order-book data
remains an important direction for future work.

\subsection{Post-Freeze Validation Updates}
\label{sec:postfreeze}

After the frozen paper pipeline was completed, subsequent research sessions in the
parent repository added three extensions that are not part of the compact
reproducibility workflow here, but that strengthen the paper's interpretation.

\input{../output/tables/tab_postfreeze}

First, a dedicated executable holding-period grid tested whether a slower hold
would outperform daily rebalancing once the overnight gap was removed from the
return definition. It did not: daily rebalancing remained optimal even in the
strict T+1-open executable framework. In the no-VIX supplemental rerun, annual
net at $0.30\%$ RT is $+59.32\%$ for daily rebalancing, versus $+44.60\%$ for
$H=2$ and $+21.66\%$ for $H=5$, reinforcing the claim that the signal decays
rapidly and is genuinely short-horizon.

Second, a later no-VIX production variant was subjected to explicit cost stress.
Its exact break-even point was approximately $0.54\%$ RT, close to the frozen
pipeline's $\approx 0.58\%$ estimate. The qualitative conclusion is unchanged:
the signal is economically meaningful, but transaction costs are a first-order
constraint.

Third, a later 2026 live-validation rerun on the no-VIX production variant
preserved IC at $+0.1323$ with positive gross returns. The square-weighted
portfolio earned $+0.198\%$ gross per trade, but remained slightly negative
net of the assumed $0.30\%$ RT cost ($-0.102\%$ per trade). This strengthens
the honest interpretation of the 2026 evidence: the signal remains alive, but
realized P\&L is still friction-sensitive.

\subsection{Liquidity and Scalability}
The top-5 selection is drawn from a 393-stock universe, with the model frequently
selecting mid- and small-cap names where the cross-sectional signal is strongest
(consistent with the H61 liquidity filter result: restricting to the top-50 most
liquid stocks approximately halves IC). This implies the strategy is most effective
at retail scale (sub-billion VND AUM) and degrades as position sizes approach a
meaningful fraction of daily turnover.

\subsection{Signal Stability and Monitoring}
\label{sec:stability}
The signal has been stable across the observed sample period (2015--2026). As
institutional participation on HOSE grows and market microstructure matures,
the opening auction dynamics hypothesized in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism} may
attenuate. We recommend monitoring annual IC against a predetermined retraining
threshold ($\text{IC} < 0.05$ for two consecutive years would warrant model review).

\subsection{Future Directions}
Several extensions are natural: incorporating intraday order-book features to
test the opening auction hypothesis directly; sector-specific sub-models to exploit
the within-sector stock-picking structure identified in Section~\ref{sec:sector};
and extension to the HNX and UpCoM exchanges, which share the retail-dominated
microstructure of HOSE but have received no attention in this study.

% ── 8. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have documented short-horizon cross-sectional return predictability in Vietnamese
HOSE equities. Using XGBoost with 35 price-based features, strict walk-forward
validation across 393 stocks and 11 years, and four pre-planned robustness tests,
we find:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Mean daily IC $= +0.1515$, positive and significant in 11/11 years
  \item Top-5 square-weighted portfolio: gross $+0.575\%$ / net $+0.275\%$ per trade,
        net Sharpe $\approx 2.19$ under idealized execution (concentrated, retail-scale)
  \item 4/4 robustness tests consistent with a genuine signal
  \item 2026 holdout IC $= +0.1343$: strong ranking evidence, but only a weak-pass
        economic result because partial-year net performance remains cost-sensitive
\end{itemize}

The signal is concentrated at the $T+1$ open, is not explained by sector timing,
is not a disguised mean-reversion rule, and has remained stable over the observed
sample period. We hypothesize the underlying mechanism is delayed cross-sectional
price adjustment in HOSE's retail-dominated opening auction.
Direct testing of this mechanism through intraday order-book data represents the
primary avenue for future work.

\section{Full Feature List}
\label{app:features}

All 35 features are computed from daily OHLCV data and the VNINDEX close price.
No fundamentals, news, or alternative data are used.

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{MA distance} (4):] $\text{ma\_dist}_{w} = \text{close}/\text{MA}(w) - 1$
       for $w \in \{5, 10, 20, 50\}$
  \item[\textbf{RSI} (1):] 14-day Wilder RSI
  \item[\textbf{MACD} (3):] Price-normalized MACD line, signal line, histogram (12,26,9)
  \item[\textbf{Bollinger} (2):] Band width $(U-L)/\text{MA}$ and band position
       $(P-L)/(U-L)$ (20-day, $2\sigma$)
  \item[\textbf{ATR} (1):] 14-day average true range normalized by close
  \item[\textbf{Candle structure} (4):] Body ratio, upper/lower wick ratios, overnight gap
  \item[\textbf{Stochastic} (2):] 14-day \%K and 3-day \%D
  \item[\textbf{ADX} (2):] 14-day ADX trend strength; direction indicator
       ($+\text{DI} - {-}\text{DI}$)
  \item[\textbf{Realized volatility} (4):] Rolling std of daily returns for
       $w \in \{5,10,20\}$; intraday range $(H-L)/C$
  \item[\textbf{Relative return} (6):] Stock excess return vs VNINDEX for lags
       $\ell \in \{1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 20\}$ days
  \item[\textbf{Market return} (6):] Raw VNINDEX return for lags
       $\ell \in \{1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 20\}$ days (market context features)
\end{description}
